Legislation on the Move

Our representatives are spending a large proportion of their budget on travel.

Data from ProPublica

Of the categories on which representatives spend their budgets, personnel compensation and travel are the top two overall.

When taking into account that each representative has a certain amount of staffers and contractors, each paid a large amount yearly, the next most interesting category becomes travel.


The top 10 travel spenders are largely from red states, but surprisingly, a fair amount of Democrats are also big travel spenders.

This mostly comes down to location.

You might be wondering, though: what exactly are these representatives spending all that travel money on?

The top answer is gas mileage reimbursement, which makes sense given that many of the top spenders are from large states like Texas. But then there are expenditures like meals and hotels.

You could make the argument that if being a member of Congress is like being hired at a company, then representatives should have access to funds needed in order to do their jobs, but for the most part, oversight is limited. We the people, as voting HR, don't actually have the ability to fire our representatives.

But looking at it further, there's a time for traveling, too: in the last two years, our representatives have stopped spending as much in the first and second quarter of the year.

In 2018, there was a midterm election that completely changed the way the board was drawn; the House flipped from a majority Republican to a majority Democrat construction.

You can pretty clearly see that representatives spent a large chunk of that year campaigning; they're actually not allowed to use their budget for campaigning, which is why they usually have separate campaign funds. However, if representatives are spending such a huge portion of the year trying to figure out how to get re-elected, when do they have time to do the job they were hired for?
